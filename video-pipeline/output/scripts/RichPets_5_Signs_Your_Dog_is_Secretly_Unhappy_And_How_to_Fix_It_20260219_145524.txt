**1. Changes in Sleep Patterns**

[VISUAL: Dog sleeping excessively in various locations - on the couch, in their bed, on the floor]

Is your normally energetic pup suddenly sleeping *way* more than usual? Or, conversely, are they restless and pacing at night? Drastic changes in sleep patterns can be a major red flag.

According to the American Kennel Club, a healthy adult dog sleeps around 12-14 hours a day. More than that *could* signal underlying unhappiness or even a health issue. So, pay attention to their sleeping habits!

[VISUAL: Split screen - one side shows a dog sleeping peacefully, the other shows a dog pacing restlessly]

*How to fix it:* First, rule out any medical causes with a vet checkup. If they get a clean bill of health, try increasing their daily exercise. A tired dog is often a happier dog. Introduce new toys or puzzles to stimulate their mind, too. A bored dog can get very unhappy.

**2. Loss of Appetite or Picky Eating**

[VISUAL: Dog turning away from a full food bowl, looking disinterested. Then, show a dog selectively eating certain pieces of food and leaving the rest]

Suddenly turning their nose up at their favorite kibble? Becoming incredibly picky about what they'll eat? A decreased appetite or finicky eating habits can point to stress, anxiety, or even depression in dogs.

Sometimes it's as simple as a change in food brand, but it's often more complex.

[VISUAL: Dog being offered a treat and hesitating, then reluctantly taking it]

*How to fix it:* Rule out dental problems! Pain can definitely affect appetite. If their teeth are fine, consider the environment. Are there loud noises during feeding time? Is another pet bullying them around their food? Try feeding them in a quiet, safe space. You can also try warming up their food slightly to enhance the aroma and make it more appealing.

**3. Excessive Licking or Chewing**

[VISUAL: Close-up shots of a dog excessively licking their paws, legs, or other body parts. Then, show a dog chewing on furniture or other inappropriate items]

While grooming is normal, excessive licking or chewing can become a compulsive behavior triggered by stress or anxiety. Think of it as the dog equivalent of nail-biting!

According to a study published in the Journal of Veterinary Behavior, compulsive licking can be a sign of separation anxiety or boredom. But here's where it gets interesting: it can also be a sign of pain, such as allergies or arthritis!

[VISUAL: A dog wearing a cone of shame to prevent licking an injury]

*How to fix it:* Consult your vet to rule out medical issues like allergies or skin conditions. If it's behavioral, increase their mental and physical stimulation with more walks, playtime, and puzzle toys. If separation anxiety is the culprit, consider professional training or anxiety aids recommended by your vet, such as a calming diffuser.

**4. Reduced Playfulness or Social Interaction**

[VISUAL: A dog sitting alone while other dogs play together. Then, show a dog ignoring a toy or owner's attempts to engage in play.]

Is your once-outgoing dog now withdrawn and uninterested in playing? Are they avoiding interaction with you or other dogs? A noticeable decrease in playfulness and social interaction is a huge warning sign.

This can be subtle, so you really need to know your dog. Are they usually jumping for joy when you grab their leash, but now they just sit there?

[VISUAL: Side-by-side comparison: A dog happily wagging its tail and running towards the camera, then a dog with a tucked tail and lowered head walking slowly]

*How to fix it:* Make playtime more enticing! Try new toys, games, or even just vary the time and location of your walks. Reintroduce them to familiar dog friends in a controlled environment. It's about reigniting their spark! Positive reinforcement is key. Reward them for even small interactions.

**5. Changes in Body Language**

[VISUAL: Montage of various dog body language signals: tucked tail, whale eye (showing whites of eyes), lip licking, yawning when not tired, ears pinned back]

This is the big one, and number one might surprise you with just how many subtle cues there are! Learning to "speak dog" is crucial. A tucked tail, flattened ears, showing the whites of their eyes (whale eye), excessive lip licking, or yawning when they're not tired can all indicate stress or discomfort.

Even subtle changes like stiffness in their gait or a reluctance to be touched in certain areas can signal underlying pain or unhappiness.

[VISUAL: Close-up shot of a dog's face, showing a worried expression. Then, show the same dog looking relaxed and happy.]

*How to fix it:* Pay close attention to their body language in different situations. Identify the triggers that seem to cause them stress. Then, work to avoid or minimize those triggers. Create a safe and comfortable environment for them. If you aren't sure what's causing it, consult with a certified dog trainer or behaviorist. They can help you decipher your dog's body language and create a tailored plan to address their needs. Remember, they can't verbally tell you what's wrong!
